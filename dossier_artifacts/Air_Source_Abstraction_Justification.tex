\section{What We Changed and Why}

Our Design Concept specifies a dedicated \textbf{air compressor} delivering airflow through ducting to the ridge-mounted nozzle carriage. For the prototype, we used a \textbf{Dyson hair dryer} on max airflow (cool mode) instead. This document explains why that substitution is valid.

\section{What We're Actually Testing}

The prototype verifies two things:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Can the track move the carriage reliably?} This has nothing to do with the air source.
    \item \textbf{Does a planar slit nozzle clear better than a circular one?} This depends on the flow characteristics \textit{at the nozzle exit}, not on what's generating the flow upstream.
\end{enumerate}

Picking the right compressor is a component selection problem (match the CFM/PSI spec), not a design question. Spending budget and time on plumbing a compressor would not have tested anything new. The Dyson gives us continuous, high-volume airflow so we can focus on the nozzle geometry, which is the actual design variable we need to verify.

We originally planned to use a ball pump, but quickly realized it only produces pulsed, low-volume bursts. You can't create a sustained boundary-layer shear with a ball pump. The team decided to switch to the Dyson early in Phase 3 because it provides the continuous flow regime that the final compressor will also provide.

The important thing is continuous, steady-state volumetric flow through the nozzle. Both the Dyson and a compressor provide this.

\section{What This Lets Us Verify (and What It Doesn't)}

\textbf{Verified with the Dyson:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Planar slit vs.\ circular nozzle comparison at equivalent input flow
    \item 30$^\circ$ angle of attack optimization (Coanda effect, $F_x$/$F_y$ balance)
    \item Wet debris clearing at distance (the stiction stress test)
    \item Force conservation across nozzle geometries (mass-flow conservation holds)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Left for the next phase (needs the real compressor):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Final CFM/PSI specification
    \item Noise compliance (O-3.1-STRU-1: $<$70\,dBA at 3\,m). The Dyson is too loud, but it's not the production air source
    \item Pressure losses through the ducting from compressor to ridge
    \item Weatherproofing the air supply path
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary}

The standard prototyping approach is to isolate the design variable from commodity components. The nozzle geometry is our novel contribution; the air source is off-the-shelf. The Dyson lets us test what matters without burning budget on something we'd just be buying, not designing.
