Some more iterations: List of Changes During Final Integration
1. Motor Substitution: The drivetrain replaced a previously considered direct-drive DC motor approach, which was abandoned due to an inability to perform accurate time-based dead reckoning. By utilizing a Nema 17 stepper architecture, the microcontroller can command an exact number of steps, translating to millimeter-perfect linear actuation regardless of the variable mass or friction of the carriage. (Reference Artifact: Iteration 1 - DC Motor/Breadboard) 2. Circuit Evolution (H-Bridge to A4988): Initial integration attempts utilized a standard L298N H-Bridge to drive the DC motor. However, due to the high mass of the carriage and the friction of the continuous track, the H-Bridge suffered extreme voltage drops, resulting in motor stalling and severe jitter. The final integration completely replaced this architecture with an A4988 Stepper Driver and screw-terminal power distribution blocks. This eliminated the jitter and provided the necessary micro-stepping torque. 	4	Thermal Tuning:
	2	To ensure the system could run continuously without entering thermal shutdown, the A4988 driver's current-limiting potentiometer ($V_{REF}$) was manually tuned back to approximately 50% capacity, capping the current to prevent the IC from overheating while maintaining holding torque.
	5	Firmware State-Machine Integration:
	3	The CircuitPython logic was upgraded from simple time-based delays to a reactive, interrupt-style state machine. The firmware now actively polls the hardware limit switches (GP15 and GP16) via internal pull-up resistors. When a switch is driven LOW, the logic instantly reverses the A4988 DIR pin rather than waiting for a predefined time loop to finish. This guarantees the carriage will not overrun the track and break the timing belt.
	6	Hardware Deadzone Verification:
	4	During final assembly, we realized the physical limit switches occupied ~2cm of track space on either end, creating a mechanical "deadzone." To compensate, the final integration involved intentionally overhanging the nozzle by 2cm on either side of the carriage mount, guaranteeing 100% surface clearing despite the mechanical travel limits.
